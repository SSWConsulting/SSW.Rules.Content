The word "but" often sounds negative and creates friction, even when you're agreeing. Phrases like *"Yes, but..."* can come across as dismissive, subtly negating what was just said. This can make the other person feel unheard or contradicted.

Instead, try using *"Yes, and..."* to acknowledge the previous point and build upon it. This approach encourages collaboration and keeps conversations constructive.

This small change keeps the tone neutral and professional while delivering the same message more effectively.

## 💼 Sales Tip

When a client questions the price, don’t say:

<boxEmbed
  style="greybox"
  body={<>
    “Yes, but it’s expensive because we put a lot of effort into it.”
  </>}
  figurePrefix="bad"
  figure="Sounds apologetic and defensive."
/>

Instead, use confidence and the “yes, and” approach:

<boxEmbed
  style="greybox"
  body={<>
    “Yes, and that’s because we put a lot of effort into it.”
  </>}
  figurePrefix="good"
  figure="Acknowledges the concern and reinforces the value."
/>

Stand behind your value. The language you use shapes how your price is perceived.

## Handling objections and personal attacks

Sometimes the other person is flat-out wrong or takes a swipe at you.

Use **Acknowledge - Validate - Reframe (AVR)** to stay calm, correct the record, and move forward:

| Step                                           | What to say                                                                       | Why it works                                                                        |
| ---------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **1 Acknowledge**                              | "I hear this is frustrating."                                                     | Naming feelings lowers defensiveness and reduces emotional arousal                  |
| **2 Validate**<br />*(Feel-Felt-Found option)* | "Others **felt** the same at first…"                                              | People feel heard when you show empathy                                             |
| **3 Reframe with 'Yes, and'**                  | "Yes, **and** it sounds like the real issue is risk - let's look at mitigations." | Improv research shows "Yes, and" increases cooperation and creative problem-solving |
| **4 Provide facts calmly**                     | "Our last three projects this size finished inside two weeks."                    | Non-violent communication focuses on needs, not blame                               |
| **5 Bridge and pivot**                         | "What might help is a weekly checkpoint - does that work for you?"                | Bridging keeps the conversation moving forward instead of getting stuck in debate   |

### Quick scripts examples

#### Price objection (sales call)

**Prospect:** *“That’s double our budget!”*\
**Answer:** *“I get why that feels steep, and others felt the same. They found bundling discovery and the build cut re-work. Shall we unpack the value together?”*

#### Personal dig (internal)

**Colleague:** *“You always set impossible deadlines.”*\
**Answer:** *“Sounds like the timing hasn’t worked for you, and I’d like to fix that. Let’s map the timeline and see where it slipped.”*

## Why this works

* Validation makes people feel heard, reducing stress and creating psychological safety
* Reframing steers the conversation from blame to collaborative problem-solving
* Bridging and pivoting are proven objection-handling techniques used in professional sales and negotiation

## Cheatsheet

* Swap **"but"** for **"and"** or a new sentence
* AVR for heat: **Acknowledge, Validate, Reframe**
* Use **Feel-Felt-Found** if you need a quick empathy shortcut
* End with a forward-looking question. E.g. *"How does that sound?"*
