The primary objective of most meetings should be to establish clear next steps. These steps should be broken down into action items and assigned to the responsible individuals, with all other attendees CC’d.

<endIntro />

## Designate someone to be the scribe

The scribe's purpose is to draft action points during the meeting and **assigns them directly to those responsible for completing the tasks** to ensure action points brought up during the meeting do not get forgotten. 

The scribe should also document any decision-making process by writing down each person's arguments. 

<boxEmbed
  body={<>
    **Warning:** The scribe should **not** be the person who is presenting.
  </>}
  figurePrefix="none"
  figure=""
  style="warning"
/>

After writing down everyone’s comments on the options, the notes can be reviewed as a group to better inform what the best course of action might be. If the scribe hasn't been designated at the start, simply ask: *"Who will be the scribe?"*, or assign it directly to someone.

The scribe is resposible to:

* Document the outcomes from the meeting’s agenda
* Documenting the [items that may be off-topic](https://www.ssw.com.au/rules/keep-track-of-a-parking-lot-for-topics) and require more discussion by subsets of the meeting’s attendees (aka the "**parking lot**")
* Ad hoc tasks that come up from brainstorming, usually sent as separate email tasks or created as PBIs in a backlog
* Scheduling the next meeting

## Capturing the action items

There are several effective ways to capture action items and notes during a meeting. Here are the recommended options:

### Option 1: YakShaver (⭐️ recommended)

[YakShaver](https://yakshaver.ai/) turns meeting recordings into clear emails or PBIs, assigns the right tasks, and notifies the right people using CCs or @mentions.

### Option 2: Microsoft Teams ‘Meeting Notes’ tab

In a scheduled Teams meeting, the **Notes** tab is the ideal place to record information, as it keeps everything connected to the meeting’s context.

<imageEmbed alt="Image" size="large" showBorder={true} figurePrefix="good" figure="Use the Notes tab during the meeting to track assignments" src="/uploads/rules/creating-action-items/MicrosoftTeams-Meeting-Notes.jpeg" />

The meeting organizer should allow time after the meeting to check the action points before their next meeting. This can seen as a summary page at the end of the meeting.

<imageEmbed alt="Image" size="large" showBorder={true} figurePrefix="good" figure="Review the meeting summary for follow-ups" src="/uploads/rules/creating-action-items/Meeting-Notes-Tasks.jpeg" />

### Option 3: Quick and dirty

During the call, type your key action items or decisions directly into a Teams message.

Once you've noted everything down, ask everyone in the call to "Like" 👍 the message if they agree with the summary.

This helps ensure consensus and provides a quick way to confirm that everyone is on the same page without the overhead of creating a new Loop.

### Option 4: Loop it up

For more detailed or complex notes, create a new Loop page and share the link with the group.

As the discussion progresses, use the Loop to collaboratively edit and refine the notes.

After the meeting, once you’ve created PBIs (Product Backlog Items) or other tasks based on the notes, include a link to the corresponding PBIs and remember to [“zz” (mark as obsolete)](https://www.ssw.com.au/rules/zz-files) the Loop notes.

This prevents anyone from accidentally editing the old notes without realizing that a newer, finalized version exists.
