A team starts designing screens before understanding the product goals, users, or constraints. Halfway through development, requirements change, edge cases appear, and everyone realizes the UI doesn’t match the real-world workflow. This leads to rework, design debt, and frustrated stakeholders.

<endIntro />

## Step 1 — Understand the Product & Business Context

Before discussing screens, get clear on why the product exists and what “good” looks like.

In the initial kick-off, aim to answer these early:

### Product purpose

* What problem does this product solve – and for whom?
* Why does it exist?
* What business goal does success achieve?
* What does leadership care about most? (Speed? Sales? Efficiency? Compliance?)

### Success metrics

* How will success be measured?
* What does a “win” look like 3–6 months after launch?
* Are there KPIs tied to usage, revenue, or adoption?

### Deadlines & constraints

* Is there a fixed deadline?
* Are there regulatory or compliance requirements?
* Are there technical or architecture constraints?
* What constraints are flexible? What are the non-negotiables?
* If you don’t understand business priorities, you can’t prioritize UX decisions properly.

## Step 2 — Identify the Users (Never Skip This)

Design scope depends heavily on who is using the system.

Capture:

| Area	             | Questions                                    |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| User types        | Internal staff, brokers, customers, admins?  |
| User roles        | What permissions differ?                     |
| Technical ability | Highly technical or non-technical?           |
| Frequency of use  | Daily operational tool or occasional system? |
| Environment       | Desktop? Mobile? Field work? Low bandwidth?  |

👉 If we don’t know the users, we can’t know how much UX is required.

An internal admin tool used daily by trained staff requires very different UX effort compared to a customer-facing public product.

## Step 3 — Understand the Current Experience

We must know what we are replacing or evolving.

Ask:

* Is there an existing system?
* What works well today?
* What frustrates users?
* What must stay the same?
* What must change?

This helps identify:

UX risk areas

* Migration complexity
* Information architecture needs
* Emotional resistance to change
* If users already have habits, your design must respect them.

## Step 4 — Determine the Design Complexity Level

Not every project needs the same depth of design.

### 🟢 Low UI Complexity

#### Characteristics:

* Internal tools
* Few screens
* Known workflows
* Existing design system

#### Typical Deliverables:

* Wireframes
* Light UI polish
* Close dev collaboration

### 🟡 Medium UI Complexity

#### Characteristics:

* New workflows
* Multiple user roles
* Moderate branding needs
* Typical Deliverables:

#### Information architecture

* User flows
* Wireframes
* High-fidelity UI
* Component decisions
* Structured dev handoff

### 🔴 High UX Complexity

#### Characteristics:

* Customer-facing product
* New product definition
* Unknown workflows
* Competitive market

### Typical Deliverables:

* Discovery workshops
* Research & personas
* Journey mapping
* Prototyping and testing
* Full design system alignment

Over-designing wastes time. Under-designing causes rework.

The review determines the correct level of depth.

## Step 5 — Confirm Scope in Plain Language

Before starting UI work, summarize clearly:

We are designing:

* Which users
* Which workflows
* Which screens
* Which level of fidelity
* Which deliverables

This avoids the classic:

> “I thought design included that…”

Alignment early is cheaper than correction later.

## The Outcome

A proper design spec review:

* Reduces ambiguity
* Improves estimation accuracy
* Prevents scope creep
* Aligns business and UX goals
* Makes developers more efficient
* Good UI is not about making things look nice.

It’s about designing the right solution at the right level of effort.
