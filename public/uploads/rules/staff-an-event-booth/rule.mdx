Sponsoring a booth costs thousands of dollars - in fees, swag, travel, and the time of the people manning it. The booth itself doesn't generate leads; the people standing at it do. Done well, a booth is the start of dozens of warm conversations. Done badly, it's an expensive piece of furniture that attendees walk past.

## Before the event

Preparation is what separates a booth that *engages* from a booth that just *exists*. By the time doors open, you should know:

* **The event** - the agenda, the keynote topics, the social events, where the coffee is
* **The speakers** - who's presenting, on what, and where SSW staff are speaking
* **The sponsors** - who else is there, especially partners and clients you might want to introduce attendees to
* **Your demo** - what you're showing, what to click, what to say if it breaks
* **The giveaways** - what swag you have, how much, and any rules around it (e.g. one per attendee, raffle entry required)

If an attendee asks "what's on after lunch?" and you fumble, you've signalled that you're not really part of the event - just a vendor parked in the corner.

## Booth presentation

The physical setup signals "open for business" or "leave us alone" before anyone says a word.

* **Banner placement** - visible from the main walkway, not hidden behind your laptop
* **Front edge clear** - no laptops, food, water bottles, or bags blocking the table edge attendees would lean on
* **Swag visible but not blocking** - within reach of the attendee, not piled up like a fortress
* **Nobody sitting behind the table** - it creates a "checkpoint" feel that kills approachability

## Body language and posture

Your body language is doing more work than your banner.

* **Stand, don't sit** - sitting signals "off duty". Standing at the front edge invites approach
* **Phone away** - eyes on the floor, not the screen. A phone in hand reads as "do not disturb"
* **Don't cluster** - two staff chatting to each other looks like a private conversation. Spread out and face outward
* **Smile and make eye contact** - the universal "you can talk to me" signal

The opener decides whether the next 90 seconds happen at all.

Other openers that work:

* **Reference their lanyard** - "Oh, you're from {{ COMPANY }} - I saw this {{ PROJECT }} you guys were working on, how does {{ X }} work?"
* **Ask why they're here** - "What brings you to the conference?" beats "Are you looking for consulting?"
* **Comment on a session** - "Did you catch the keynote? What did you think?"

And read the room - slow walkers and lanyard-readers are warm; fast walkers with headphones are not. Don't chase.

## Running the conversation

Once they've stopped, the goal isn't to *sell* - it's to *qualify and connect*.

* **Listen more than you talk** - the attendee's problem is more interesting than your product
* **Qualify gently** - "What kind of work does your team do?" before "Are you in the market for consulting?"
* **Hand off, don't hoard** - if they need deep technical or commercial detail, introduce a teammate by name. Never say "that's not my area" without immediately pointing to who can help
* **Know your limits** - it's better to say "let me connect you with \[name] who knows this inside out" than to bluff

If the conversation moves toward "what does your product actually do?", that's where the [Pitch a Product](/pitch-a-product) rule takes over - have your 60-second pitch ready.

## Capturing the lead

A great conversation with no record of it is a wasted conversation.

* **Capture while they're standing there** - business card, QR code to a form, or a follow-up promise written down. Not "I'll remember"
* **Note one specific thing** - what they're working on, what they asked about, what they're attending next. Generic notes are useless when you're following up three days later
* **Confirm the channel** - "I'll email you Monday with \[thing]" is a commitment; "I'll be in touch" is not

## The graceful exit

Both you and the attendee need a clean way out so the next conversation can start.

* **Acknowledge and close** - "Great chatting - grab a sticker on your way and I'll send that link Monday"
* **Don't stretch it** - if the conversation has wound down, end it. Holding someone hostage at your booth costs you the next three attendees walking past

## Swag strategy

Swag is a tool, not a transaction.

* **Give it for a conversation** - the goal is the chat, not the giveaway
* **Tier it** - stickers and pens for everyone, premium items (bottles, shirts) for qualified conversations
* **Don't leave it for self-service** - you've just turned your booth into a vending machine and lost every conversation it would have started

## Sustaining energy

A booth is a marathon, not a sprint. Tired staff equals a dead booth.

* **Rotate every 60-90 minutes** - swap shifts so nobody burns out
* **Reset between waves** - tidy swag, restock cards, wipe the demo screen
* **Eat and hydrate off-shift** - never at the booth itself
* **Brief the incoming shift** - "we've handed out 40 cards, the demo is on slide 3, \[attendee] is coming back at 2pm"

## After the event

The event ends; the work doesn't.

* **Same-day debrief** - what worked, who to follow up, what to fix next time. Memories fade fast
* **LinkedIn within 48 hours** - while you're still recognisable. "Great to meet you at \[event] - here's that link I mentioned"
* **CRM next, not eventually** - lead cards belong in your CRM, not in someone's backpack
* **Send the promised thing** - if you said "I'll email you Monday", email them Monday. The follow-through is the differentiator

Treat the booth like an [initial meeting](/prepare-for-initial-meetings) - first impressions matter most, and preparation is what makes them count.
