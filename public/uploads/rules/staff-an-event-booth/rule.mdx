Getting a booth costs a lot - in fees, merch, travel, as well as the time of the people staffing the booth itself. The existence of the booth, however, doesn't generate leads; the people running it do. When done well, a booth is the starting point of dozens of warm conversations. Done poorly, it's nothing more than an expensive piece of furniture that the attendees walk past.

## Preparation going into the event

**Preparation** is the key factor that separates a booth that **just exists** from one that **engages** people. For example, if an attendee asks, “what's on after lunch?” and you have no idea, you've just signalled that you're not really a part of the event - just a vendor parked in the corner.

When its time for the doors to open, you should know the agenda, keynote topics, who's speaking and their topics (especially the slots of your peers), as well as the other sponsors that are in the room. You should also know your demo from end-to-end - what to click, what to do if it breaks - and the rules around giveaways (e.g. one per attendee, raffle entry, etc.)

A quick 5-minute walk and scouting of the venue before doors open is one of the highest-value things that you can do. You can find out where the coffee is, where the bathrooms are, and which way foot traffic flows past your booth.

## Booth setup and presentation

The **physical setup** of a booth signals “open for business” or “leave us alone” long before anyone says a word. The most common mistake is a wall of laptops, water bottles, and bags piled along the front edge of the table - taking the exact spot an attendee would lean on to start a conversation.

Your **banner** needs to be **visible from the main walkway**, not hidden behind your laptop. Available merch should be visible and within easy reach of the attendee, not piled up like a fortress that the visitor has to lean over. Nobody should be sitting behind the table - it creates a "checkpoint" feel that kills approachability.

## Body language and posture

Whilst banner branding is important, its the people that lead to real conversations - your body language itself does more than any banner could. Stand, don't sit - sitting signals "off duty", while standing at the front edge invites approach. Keep your phone away; eyes on the floor, not the screen. A phone in hand reads as "do not disturb".

If you're working the booth with a teammate, resist the urge to chat to each other to pass the time. Two staff in conversation looks like a private meeting that an attendee would feel rude interrupting. Instead, you should try to spread out, face outward, and make eye contact with the people walking past. A smile is the universal "you can talk to me" signal.

## Starting the conversation

The opener decides whether the next 90 seconds happen at all. The single biggest mistake is "Can I help you?" - it's a closed question, and the closed answer is "No thanks, just looking", followed by the attendee walking away.

A good opener is specific and gives the attendee something easy to say yes or no to. Anything that opens a door is better than anything that asks them to commit before they know what's behind it.

Other openers that work:

* **Have an interesting activity** - People inevitably get tired from multiple conversations throughout the day, having an interesting activity gives them something to break up the monotony of constant presentations and provides an interesting starting point for a conversation.
* **Reference their lanyard** - "Oh, you're from &#123;&#123; COMPANY &#125;&#125; - I saw this &#123;&#123; PROJECT &#125;&#125; you guys were working on, how does &#123;&#123; FEATURE &#125;&#125; work?"
* **Ask why they're here** - "What brings you to the conference - any particular topics you're focused on?" beats "Are you looking for consulting?"
* **Comment on a session** - "Did you catch the keynote? What did you think?"

And always read the room - slow walkers and lanyard-readers are warm; fast walkers with headphones are not. Don't chase conversations that aren't there.

## Running the conversation

Once they've stopped, the goal isn't to *sell* - it's to \*qualify and connect \*with the people.

* **Listen more than you talk** - the attendee's problem is more interesting than your product
* **Qualify gently** - "What kind of work does your team do?" before "Are you in the market for consulting?"
* **Hand off, don't hoard** - if they need deep technical or commercial detail, introduce a teammate by name. Never say "that's not my area" without immediately pointing to who can help
* **Know your limits** - it's better to say "&#123;&#123; TEAMMATE &#125;&#125; knows this inside out, let me grab them" than to overextend in an area you're not comfortable with

## Capturing the lead

No matter how great a conversation, with no record of it, it risks becoming a wasted conversation as you will inevitably have far too many conversations over the course of staffing the booth to remember each and every person you've spoken with. You should instead:

* **Capture while they're standing there** - business card, QR code to a form, or a follow-up promise written down. Not "I'll remember"
* **Note one specific thing** - whether it be what they're working on, what they asked about, what they're attending next. Generic notes are useless when you're following up three days later
* **Confirm the channel** - "I'll email you Monday with &#123;&#123; ITEM &#125;&#125;" is a commitment; "I'll be in touch" is not

## The graceful exit

Both you and the attendee need a clean way out so the next conversation can start.

* **Acknowledge and close** - "Great chatting - grab a sticker on your way and I'll send that link Monday"
* **Don't stretch it** - if the conversation has wound down, end it. Holding someone hostage at your booth costs you the next three attendees walking past

## Merch strategy

Merch is a tool, not a transaction - you're not there to sell merch, but to use it:

* **Give it for a conversation** - the goal is the chat, not the giveaway
* **Tier it** - stickers and pens can be for everyone, whereas premium items (bottles, shirts) for qualified conversations or raffles
* **Don't leave it for self-service** - doing so would just turn your booth into a vending machine and lose every conversation it would have started

## Sustaining energy throughout

Running an event booth is a marathon, not a sprint. Staff that are tired inevitably leads to a dead booth without energy or personality. In order to maintain energy throughout the event, you should always:

* **Rotate every 60-90 minutes** - swap shifts and alternate so nobody burns out
* **Reset between waves** - tidy up merch, restock business cards, wipe demo screens
* **Eat and hydrate off-shift** - you should do so after your shift staffing the booth, but never at the booth itself, as often people don't want to interrupt and feel rude doing so
* **Brief the incoming shift** - brief your peers with some information on what to expect, for example, "we've handed out 40 cards, the demo is on slide 3, &#123;&#123; ATTENDEE &#125;&#125; mentioned they were coming back at 2pm"

## After the event

The event ends; the work doesn't.

* **Run a retrospective** - what worked, who to follow up, what to fix next time. Memories fade fast, for more details, checkout our rule on [Do you do a retrospective after an activity ends?](https://www.ssw.com.au/rules/do-a-retrospective)
* **Follow-ups within 48 hours** - “Strike while the iron is hot”, you should send off promised follow ups to people while you're still recognisable. For example, "Great to meet you at &#123;&#123; EVENT &#125;&#125; - here's that link I mentioned"
