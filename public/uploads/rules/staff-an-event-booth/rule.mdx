Sponsoring a booth costs thousands of dollars - in fees, swag, travel, and the time of the people manning it. The booth itself doesn't generate leads; the people standing at it do. Done well, a booth is the start of dozens of warm conversations. Done badly, it's an expensive piece of furniture that attendees walk past.

## Before the event

Preparation is what separates a booth that *engages* from a booth that just *exists*. If an attendee asks "what's on after lunch?" and you fumble, you've signalled that you're not really part of the event - just a vendor parked in the corner.

By the time doors open, you should know the agenda, the keynote topics, who's speaking and on what, where the SSW staff slots are, and which other sponsors are in the room. You should also know your demo cold - what to click, what to say if it breaks - and the rules around your giveaways (one per attendee, raffle entry required, premium swag for qualified leads only).

A 5-minute walk of the venue before doors open is one of the highest-value things you can do. You'll know where the coffee is, where the bathrooms are, and which way the foot traffic flows past your booth.

## Booth presentation

The physical setup signals "open for business" or "leave us alone" before anyone says a word. The most common mistake is a wall of laptops, water bottles, and bags piled along the front edge of the table - the exact spot an attendee would lean on to start a conversation. Clear that edge.

Your banner needs to be visible from the main walkway, not hidden behind your laptop. Swag should be visible and within easy reach of the attendee, not piled up like a fortress that the visitor has to lean over. And nobody should be sitting behind the table - it creates a "checkpoint" feel that kills approachability.

## Body language and posture

Your body language is doing more work than your banner. Stand, don't sit - sitting signals "off duty", while standing at the front edge invites approach. Keep your phone away; eyes on the floor, not the screen. A phone in hand reads as "do not disturb".

If you're working the booth with a teammate, resist the urge to chat to each other. Two staff in conversation looks like a private meeting that an attendee would feel rude interrupting. Spread out, face outward, and make eye contact with the people walking past. A smile is the universal "you can talk to me" signal.

## Starting the conversation

The opener decides whether the next 90 seconds happen at all. The single biggest mistake is "Can I help you?" - it's a closed question, and the closed answer is "No thanks, just looking", followed by the attendee walking away.

A good opener is specific and gives the attendee something easy to say yes or no to. "Have you seen the demo yet?" works because it's a 30-second commitment, not a sales pitch. So does referencing their lanyard ("Oh, you're from &#123;&#123; COMPANY &#125;&#125; - we worked with your team on &#123;&#123; PROJECT &#125;&#125;") or asking about the conference itself ("What brings you to the event?" or "Did you catch the keynote?"). Anything that opens a door is better than anything that asks them to commit before they know what's behind it.

And read the room as people approach. Slow walkers, lanyard-readers, and people slowing as they pass your demo screen are warm. Fast walkers wearing headphones are not - don't chase them.

## Running the conversation

Once an attendee stops, the goal isn't to *sell* - it's to *qualify and connect*. The biggest signal of a confident booth is staff who listen more than they talk. The attendee's problem is more interesting than your product, and what they tell you in the first 60 seconds determines whether you should be pitching consulting, demoing a feature, or pointing them at a colleague.

Qualify gently. "What kind of work does your team do?" lands better than "Are you in the market for consulting?". And know your limits - if the attendee starts asking deep technical or commercial questions, don't bluff. Introduce a teammate by name and hand the conversation off cleanly: "&#123;&#123; TEAMMATE &#125;&#125; knows this inside out, let me grab them". Never say "that's not my area" without immediately pointing to who can help.

If the conversation moves toward "what does your product actually do?", that's where the [Pitch a Product](/pitch-a-product) rule takes over - have your 60-second pitch ready.

## Capturing the lead

A great conversation with no record of it is a wasted conversation. The most important rule of lead capture is to do it *while the attendee is standing there* - take their business card, scan a QR code to a form, or write down a follow-up promise. "I'll remember" is not a system, and three booths and a coffee later you won't.

Note one specific thing about each lead: what they're working on, the question they asked, the session they were heading to next. Generic notes ("Sarah, interested in Azure") are useless when you're following up three days later. Specific notes ("Sarah from &#123;&#123; COMPANY &#125;&#125; - migrating from on-prem to Azure, blocked on Active Directory federation, asked about our Azure rules") give you a real opener for the follow-up email.

If you commit to something, be specific. "I'll email you Monday with the link to that case study" is a commitment you can keep. "I'll be in touch" is not.

## The graceful exit

Both you and the attendee need a clean way out so the next conversation can start. A simple acknowledgement does it: "Great chatting - grab a sticker on your way and I'll send that link Monday". You've ended on a warm note, given them a small reason to remember you, and freed yourself for the next person walking past. If a conversation has wound down, end it - holding someone hostage at your booth costs you the next three attendees.

## Swag strategy

Swag is a tool, not a transaction. The point is to start a conversation, not to give people free stuff. The simplest rule is: **swag for a conversation, not a drive-by**. If your bowl of stickers is on the front edge of the table for self-service, you've turned your booth into a vending machine and lost every conversation it would have started.

Tier your swag if you can. Stickers, pens, and lollies for anyone who chats. Premium items (water bottles, branded shirts, tech accessories) reserved for qualified conversations - the attendees you'd genuinely like to follow up with. The bottle is more memorable when it's tied to a real conversation.

## Sustaining energy

A booth is a marathon, not a sprint. Tired staff means a dead booth, and a dead booth at hour 4 cancels out the leads you got in hour 1. Rotate shifts every 60 to 90 minutes so nobody burns out, and use the gap between shifts to reset - tidy the swag, restock business cards, and wipe down the demo screen.

Eat and hydrate off-shift, never at the booth itself. And brief the incoming shift like you'd hand off oncall: "we've handed out 40 cards, the demo is on slide 3, &#123;&#123; ATTENDEE &#125;&#125; from &#123;&#123; COMPANY &#125;&#125; is coming back at 2pm to chat to &#123;&#123; TEAMMATE &#125;&#125;". A good handoff turns two half-shifts into one continuous booth presence.

## After the event

The event ends; the work doesn't. The first thing to do is a same-day debrief while memory is still fresh - what worked, who to follow up with, what to fix next time. By tomorrow you'll have lost half of what you noticed.

Lead cards belong in the CRM, not in someone's backpack. Get them entered the day you're back. Then connect on LinkedIn within 48 hours while you're still recognisable - "Great to meet you at &#123;&#123; EVENT &#125;&#125; - here's that link I mentioned" is a perfectly good message.

Most importantly, send the thing you promised. If you said "I'll email you Monday", email them Monday. The follow-through is the differentiator - most booths don't do it, and the ones that do are the ones that turn booth conversations into clients.

Treat the booth like an [initial meeting](/prepare-for-initial-meetings) - first impressions matter most, and preparation is what makes them count.
