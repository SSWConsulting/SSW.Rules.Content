Unsourced assertions ask readers to take your word for it. Phrases like "studies show" or "experts agree" with no link behind them read as filler, and they give search engines and AI answer engines no way to verify what you are saying.

Backing every meaningful claim with a link to a primary or authoritative source, such as official documentation, a standards body, peer-reviewed research, or the original dataset, builds reader trust and directly supports Google's E-E-A-T signals (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness).

It also matters for discovery. AI answer engines like ChatGPT, Gemini, and Google's AI Overviews prefer content whose claims are verifiable and well-sourced. Well-cited content is far more likely to be surfaced and cited back as the answer.

<endIntro />

## Why citations matter

Citations do three jobs at once:

- **Build reader trust** - a linked source lets readers confirm a claim for themselves, which makes the whole page more credible
- **Support Google E-E-A-T** - linking out to authoritative sources is a signal of expertise and trustworthiness that Google's quality raters and algorithms reward
- **Win AI citations** - answer engines are built on retrieval and prefer claims they can trace to a reputable source, so a well-cited claim is more likely to be extracted and cited in an AI answer

## What counts as an authoritative source

Link to the closest thing to the origin of the fact:

- **Official documentation** from the vendor or maintainer
- **Standards bodies** such as W3C, IETF, or ISO
- **Peer-reviewed research** or reputable academic publications
- **Original data** from the organisation that collected it

Avoid citing a blog that cites a blog. Follow the chain back to the primary source and link that instead.

## Tips for citing well

- Link the exact page that supports the claim, not a site's home page, so readers land on the evidence
- Prefer specific figures and dates over vague generalisations
- Keep sources current, and check that the links still resolve
- Name the source in the visible text (for example "Google's web.dev research") so both humans and AI can attribute it
