Vague marketing claims like "much faster" or "industry-leading" tell the reader nothing they can verify. They read as opinion, not fact, so people discount them and AI answer engines skip right past them.

Concrete numbers do the opposite. A quantified, verifiable claim is more persuasive to a human, strengthens your E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness), and gives AI answer engines a clean, quotable fact they can lift straight into an answer and attribute to you.

Every statistic needs a source. A number with no link is just a nicer-looking assertion, so pair each figure with a link to the evidence behind it.

<endIntro />

## Replace qualitative claims with numbers

Whenever you write a claim about performance, cost, scale, or results, ask "what is the number?" If you cannot answer, the claim is not ready to publish.

Swap subjective adjectives for measured facts:

* "much faster" becomes "renders in 200ms — roughly 3× faster"
* "trusted by thousands" becomes "used by 4,200 teams"
* "saves you money" becomes "cuts hosting costs by 38%"

## Pair every statistic with its source

A number is only as trustworthy as its evidence. Link each statistic to the benchmark, study, or dataset it came from so readers — and AI answer engines — can confirm it and cite it with confidence. An uncited figure invites doubt; a cited one builds authority.

For the full detail on sourcing, see [Do you cite authoritative sources?](/cite-your-sources).

## Why this matters for AI answer engines

AI answer engines run on retrieval — they scan content for precise, extractable facts to answer a user's question. A sentence like "renders in 200ms, 3× faster than Competitor X" is exactly the kind of self-contained, attributable fact they quote. Vague claims give them nothing to work with, so your brand goes uncited.

This is a core part of [optimizing your content for AI answers](/ai-optimization-geo-aeo): the more of your claims are quantified and sourced, the more often AI tools surface and credit you.
